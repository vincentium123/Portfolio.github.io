Master's Thesis

For my thesis, I tackled a methodological question: several scientific papers published in the last few years have used deep learning methods to evaluate human attractiveness. Do the methods used generate accurate data and, if not, how can they be improved? To do this, I conducted inference using eight deep learning methods on datasets from four published studies, then replicated the studies. As the deep learning methods failed to generate accurate data, I then trained two convolutional neural networks of my own and conducted two studies, with marginally better results.

Cross-National Cultural Effects on Survey Non-response Rates

In this project, I propose a novel reason why non-response rates in surveys can vary across countries: uncertainty about the socially desirable response. Using beta regression, statistical analysis, and data visualization in R, I show that in countries where no position on socially important topics have become dominant, non-response rates to surveys are higher.

So You Want to Pet a Squirrel

This a project very seriously attempts to answer a very silly question: if you're in New York City and want to pet a squirrel, where should you go? Several years ago, a citizen science group conducted a census of all the squirrels in Central Park. Among the variables, they recorded which squirrels came close to them. Using SQL and Tableau, I analyze and visualize the data. Spoiler: it's not the lake.
