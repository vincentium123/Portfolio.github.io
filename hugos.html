The Hugo Awards

Hello and welcome to my analysis of the Hugo Awards! The Hugos are usually considered the top prize in Science Fiction, with many classics of the genre among the winners and nominees. Every year, participants at WorldCon, the world’s largest convention for print science fiction, nominate the books they consider to be the best novels of the year. After that, the five most-nominated novels are put into a final round of voting, where one is selected to win the Hugo Award. That means that every year sees four nominees and one winner (except for the handful of years where two novels tied for first).

I was interested in answering a few questions about them, so I turned to Goodreads, the world’s largest book-focused social media platform. I assembled a list of all Hugo Award winners and nominees starting in 1951 and extending up to the present day- nearly 400 novels in all. I then used Havan Agrawal’s excellent GoodreadsScraper tool to scrape data and did a bit of data cleaning using R. Once I had a clean dataset, I conducted analysis using PostgreSQL in pgAdmin 4.

The overarching question I had in mind for this analysis is pretty simple: how do the winners of the Hugo Awards compare to the nominees? There’s lots of different ways to answer that, so I’ve divided the question in multiple ways.

In order not to have giant blocks of code interspersed with the text, I’ve put links to the SQL code I wrote for each section at the bottom of each section. I’ve also made the full dataset publicly available here if you’re interested in it.

Let’s get started.

Goodreads has millions of users who can rate books on a 1 to 5 scale. This establishes a pretty good metric for both the public perception of a book and its popularity. The higher its rating, the better the public sees it; the more ratings, the more popular it is.

As an initial question, I wanted to see whether Hugo Award winners were more highly rated than nominees. To me, this gets at the entire purpose of awards - if the nominees are equally (or more highly) rated than the winners, then clearly the selection process for the Hugos differs from the general public’s idea of a good book.

In this case, there is a difference - barely. Hugo winners have an average rating of 4, while nominees have one of 3.9. While this is significant in a statistical sense, its real-world impact is fairly negligible. It does show that the Hugos do tend to produce a good set of nominees though - it isn’t a case of one good book and four mediocre ones.

While Hugo winners aren’t necessarily better than nominees, they are definitely more popular. The average winning novel has 208,807 ratings, while the average nominee has only 66,550. This holds true even if we eliminate the 15 most popular books in the list, each with over 500,000 ratings. These are largely books from popular series like Harry Potter, the Chronicles of Narnia, and Game of Thrones, plus classroom classics like Slaughterhouse-Five and Fahrenheit 451. In this case, winners still have an average of 79,900 ratings to nominees’ 26,085.

As you might have guessed, I like to read science fiction. Going into this project, I had a pretty good idea of what serious science fiction readers consider to be the best and worst Hugo winners. Hyperion, Dune, Ender’s Game, the Demolished Man, Fahrenheit 451 and many other novels are widely considered to be classics within the genre, while 1981 is often considered to be a particularly bad year. So I was interested to know about how my perception lines us with the wisdom of the crowd.

Looking at the least popular winners is a bit more interesting. Unsurprisingly, older books dominate. The others seem to be from less famous authors overall (logically). The most famous author on the list, Robert Heinlein, is an interesting case. "Farmer in the Sky" is a juvenile- the old term for a young adult book. Most likely, few young adults are searching for a fairly obscure book from 70 years ago, meaning that only hard-core sci-fi fans will be reading this book, and they simply aren't the target audience. Cyteen, by C.J. Cherryh also sticks out to me- she’s a big deal among serious sci-fi fans- but there’s a pretty simple possible explanation there: the book is massive. I own a copy that’s 1200 pages long. I’ve tried reading it twice and never finished.

Things get a bit more interesting when we look at the least and most popular years. The most popular years are interesting. 2000 and 2001 are there from Harry Potter and Game of Thrones.1970 has two classics- The Left Hand of Darkness and Slaughterhouse-Five, both widely read in high schools in the US.

What surprised me were the inclusion of 1951 and 1954, both seeming to buck the trend of older books being less popular and less regarded. Notably, these two years are unique. The books nominated weren’t voted on in 1951 and 1954, but rather 2001 and 2004. The Hugos weren’t held in these years, so sci-fi fans organized a “Retro Hugo” fifty years later. It’s likely that they picked books that had stood the test of time, resulting in higher popularity.

But it possibly didn’t matter- 1951 and 1954 were absurdly good years for sci-fi. This was near the start of the Golden Age of the genre, as the medium matured and shifted from pulp magazines to long-form novels. 1951 saw the publication of C.S. Lewis’s famous The Lion, the Witch, and the Wardrobe, still a beloved classic, as well as Jack Vance’s The Dying Earth, one of the foundational science fiction series. 1954 was even better- Ray Bradbury’s Fahrenheit 451, Arthur C. Clarke’s Childhood’s End, and Isaac Asimov’s The Caves of Steel are regarded as among the best science fiction novels of their time, and widely read today.

Now: how many years had a non-winner highest rated? In other words, how often does the public agree with the Hugos?

First things first, if the Hugos winner had no relation with the popular vote winner, we’d still expect about a 20% overlap, just by chance. There’s five nominees each year, and one wins. I've removed a few years where that wasn't the case, such as in the early 1950s when only the winner was announced in some years.

That said, the Hugo winner ends up being the highest rated book of the year 40% of the time- much higher than chance. There’s a few possible factors that explain this.

There's a few possible ways to think about this, in my opinion- and there is no need for only one to be true. One possibility is that readers rate Hugo winners higher because they have won. They see the critical consensus is behind a book, and they don't want to go against it. Another is that the Hugos are quite old, as literary awards go. Tastes have changes, novels have been re-assessed. For example, in 1966, the Hugo was awarded jointly to Dune, by Frank Herbert, and This Immortal, by Robert Zelazny. One of those is today considered one of the greatest science fiction novels ever written and is being made into a trilogy starring every famous actor (more or less) and the other one… isn’t. I’ve read This Immortal, it’s not bad, but it’s simply not near Dune’s level. A third is that the people who vote on the Hugos are a self-selected group of hard-core sci-fi fans and authors, and they have tastes that don't completely overlap with the general public.
